There's a temptation in web design to make everything look like everything else. Dark mode with neon accents. Glass morphism cards. Gradient text. The result is a sea of nearly identical portfolios and blogs.

This site was built as an exercise in restraint. The design language comes from one source: the terminal. Not as a gimmick, but as a genuine aesthetic that matches the content — technical writing about security and systems.

## Design Decisions

### Typography
Monospace fonts for headings and UI elements. System sans-serif for body text. The contrast between the two creates hierarchy without decoration.

### Color
One accent color. Everything else is derived from shades of gray. The accent is used sparingly — only for interactive elements and the eye-dot signature.

### Motion
Animations serve a purpose. The typing effect on prompts signals "this is a command." The blinking cursor signals "this is active." The CRT scanlines on the homepage add atmosphere without distraction.

### Structure
No cards. No rounded corners. No shadows. Content is presented as plain text — what it is, not what it pretends to be.

## Why Brutalism Works Here

Brutalist design isn't about being ugly. It's about being honest. A terminal-inspired aesthetic works for a security blog because it communicates the same values: function over form, clarity over decoration, substance over style.

The site still looks intentional. Every choice was made deliberately. Brutalism isn't the absence of design — it's design reduced to its essential elements.
